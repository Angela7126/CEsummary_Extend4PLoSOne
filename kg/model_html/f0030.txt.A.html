[0] Astronomy is a wonderful Grid application because datasets are inherently distributed and yet form a fairly uniform corpus. [1] In particular: [2] (1) The astronomy community has a fairly unified taxonomy, vocabulary, and codified definition of metrics and units [24]. [3] (2) Modern data is carefully peer reviewed and collected with rigorous statistical and scientific standards. [4] (3) Data provenance is tracked, and derived data sets are curated fairly carefully. [5] (4) Most data is publicly available and will remain available for the foreseeable future. [6] (5) Even though old data is much less precise than current data, old data is essential when studying time-varying phenomena. [7] Each astronomy archive covers part of the electromagnetic spectrum for a period of time and a subset of the celestial sphere. [8] All the archives are from the same sky and the same celestial objects, although different observations are made at different times. [9] Increasingly, astronomers perform multispectral studies or temporal studies combining data related to the same objects from multiple instruments and archives. [10] Cross-comparison is possible because data are well documented and schematized with a common reference frame, and have clear provenance. [11] The scale of the data -- terabytes now, petabytes soon -- means that most data must reside at archives managed by the teams that are gathering and publishing the data. [12] An astronomer wanting to study a particular kind of object or phenomenon cannot download a full copy of each archive for local processing -- both because the scientist does not have a spare local petabyte and because it would take too long to do the download. [13] Rather, the scientist must request small (gigabyte-sized now, terabyte-sized in the future) subsets from each archive that represent the few million objects of interest out of the billions at the archives.
