[0] We propose to extend the ontology of logical AI to include approximate objects, approximate predicates and approximate theories. [1] Besides the ontology we treat the relations among different approximate theories of the same phenomena. [2] Approximate predicates can't have complete if-and-only-if definitions and usually don't even have definite extensions. [3] Some approximate concepts can be refined by learning more and some by defining more and some by both, but it isn't possible in general to make them well-defined. [4] Approximate concepts are essential for representing common sense knowledge and doing common sense reasoning. [5] Assertions involving approximate concepts can be represented in mathematical logic. [6] A sentence involving an approximate concept may have a definite truth value even if the concept is ill-defined. [7] It is definite that Mount Everest was climbed in 1953 even though exactly what rock and ice is included in that mountain is ill-defined. [8] Likewise, it harms a mosquito to be swatted, although we haven't a sharp notion of what it means to harm a mosquito. [9] Whatif(x,p), which denotes what x would be like if p were true, is an important kind of approximate object. [10] The article treats successively approximate objects, approximate theories, and formalisms for describing how one object or theory approximates another.
