[0] Astronomy is a wonderful Grid application because datasets are inherently distributed and yet form a fairly uniform corpus. [1] In particular: [2] (1) The astronomy community has a fairly unified taxonomy, vocabulary, and codified definition of metrics and units [24]. [3] (2) Modern data is carefully peer reviewed and collected with rigorous statistical and scientific standards. [4] (3) Data provenance is tracked, and derived data sets are curated fairly carefully. [5] (4) Most data is publicly available and will remain available for the foreseeable future. [6] (5) Even though old data is much less precise than current data, old data is essential when studying time-varying phenomena. [7] Each astronomy archive covers part of the electromagnetic spectrum for a period of time and a subset of the celestial sphere. [8] All the archives are from the same sky and the same celestial objects, although different observations are made at different times. [9] Increasingly, astronomers perform multispectral studies or temporal studies combining data related to the same objects from multiple instruments and archives. [10] Cross-comparison is possible because data are well documented and schematized with a common reference frame, and have clear provenance. [11] The scale of the data -- terabytes now, petabytes soon -- means that most data must reside at archives managed by the teams that are gathering and publishing the data. [12] An astronomer wanting to study a particular kind of object or phenomenon cannot download a full copy of each archive for local processing -- both because the scientist does not have a spare local petabyte and because it would take too long to do the download. [13] Rather, the scientist must request small (gigabyte-sized now, terabyte-sized in the future) subsets from each archive that represent the few million objects of interest out of the billions at the archives. [14] The World Wide Telescope is an excellent Grid application. [15] It involves the federation of many distributed terascale data sources. [16] The astronomy community is interested in mining the individual datasets looking for patterns, but there is even greater interest in cross-correlating the datasets to find new phenomena. [17] The construction of each archive involves massive calculations that ingest, analyze, and categorize the instrument data, producing databases and files. [18] Cross-correlation introduces enormous requirements for data movement and computation. [19] The emergence of these huge datasets changes the roles of authors, publishers, and curators. [20] Science projects become data authors and publishers, and must also do the curatorial work that will allow archives to accept and preserve the data once the project is complete. [21] Each project will likely publish a database that is externalized as a set of Web services. [22] The astronomy community is in the midst of defining and prototyping these core services. [23] Portals will federate these Web services to provide uniform access to the archives as though they are a single distributed database. [24] Cross-identifications of objects across multiple archives, and searches involving both spatial and time-domain constraints, are typical tasks for the Virtual Observatory. [25] Because of the large sizes of the datasets, their distributed nature, and their rapidly varying content, these tasks need an architecture that is inherently distributed and scalable -- the Grid. [26] We view the Virtual Observatory as a small part of the larger effort of defining and applying an Open Grid Services Architecture and creating an international Grid infrastructure.
