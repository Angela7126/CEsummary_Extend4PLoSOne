[0] A common feature of free will is that a person has choices among alternative actions and chooses the action with the apparently most preferred consequences. [1] In a determinist theory, the mechanism that makes the choice among the alternatives is determinist. [2] The sensation of free will comes from the fact that the mechanism that generates the choices uses a non-determinist theory as a computational device and that the stage in which the choices have been identified is introspectable. [3] The present formalism is based on work in artificial intelligence (AI). [4] We present a theory of simple deterministic free will (SDFW) in a deterministic world. [5] The theory splits the mechanism that determines action into two parts. [6] The first part computes possible actions and their consequences. [7] Then the second part decides which action is most preferable and does it. [8] We formalize SDFW by two sentences in situation calculus, a mathematical logical theory often used in AI. [9] The situation calculus formalization makes the notion of free will technical. [10] According to this notion, almost no animal behavior exhibits free will, because exercising free will involves considering the consequences of alternative actions. [11] A major advantage of our notion of free will is that whether an animal does have free will may be determinable by experiment. [12] Some computer programs, e.g. [13] chess programs, exhibit SDFW. [14] Almost all do not. [15] At least SDFW seems to be required for effective chess performance and also for human-level AI. [16] Many features usually considered as properties of free will are omitted in SDFW. [17] That's what makes it simple. [18] The criterion for whether an entity uses SDFW is not behavioristic but is expressed in terms of the internal structure of the entity.
