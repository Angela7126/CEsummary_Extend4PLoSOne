[0] I have argued that we have a good story or at least good promises on many fronts, but that we have some major flaws in the story: [1] * Availability now hinges on [2] (1) Automating management so that human error is less of a factor in configuring and operating computer systems. [3] (2) Fending off hacker-attacks and other forms of sabotage. [4] This was once a remote possibility, but today most popular websites are continuously under attack from dozens of adversaries at a time. [5] Some of these adversaries are very competent and very expert. [6] * After functionality (actually getting the application to work), manageability is the dominant cost of operating most systems -- there is no Moore's law for people. [7] Yet the operations cost fall in concert with the reduced costs of hardware, software, networking, and faculties. [8] The only way to do this is to automate these tasks. [9] * Agility is a special form of functionality. [10] Agility is on the macro-level what we used to call extensibility on the micro-level. [11] * Scalability is addressed by manageability [12] So, the new role for "systems people" like you (if you got this far in the paper) and me is to focus on functionality, manageability, and defensive service. [13] Making self-managing and self-healing systems, and making systems that detect and resist sabotage stand as our greatest system building challenge.
