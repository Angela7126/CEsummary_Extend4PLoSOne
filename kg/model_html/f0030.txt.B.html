[0] The World Wide Telescope is an excellent Grid application. [1] It involves the federation of many distributed terascale data sources. [2] The astronomy community is interested in mining the individual datasets looking for patterns, but there is even greater interest in cross-correlating the datasets to find new phenomena. [3] The construction of each archive involves massive calculations that ingest, analyze, and categorize the instrument data, producing databases and files. [4] Cross-correlation introduces enormous requirements for data movement and computation. [5] The emergence of these huge datasets changes the roles of authors, publishers, and curators. [6] Science projects become data authors and publishers, and must also do the curatorial work that will allow archives to accept and preserve the data once the project is complete. [7] Each project will likely publish a database that is externalized as a set of Web services. [8] The astronomy community is in the midst of defining and prototyping these core services. [9] Portals will federate these Web services to provide uniform access to the archives as though they are a single distributed database. [10] Cross-identifications of objects across multiple archives, and searches involving both spatial and time-domain constraints, are typical tasks for the Virtual Observatory. [11] Because of the large sizes of the datasets, their distributed nature, and their rapidly varying content, these tasks need an architecture that is inherently distributed and scalable -- the Grid. [12] We view the Virtual Observatory as a small part of the larger effort of defining and applying an Open Grid Services Architecture and creating an international Grid infrastructure.
