[0] Suppose someone says, 'The dog wants to go out'. [1] The statement isn't shorthand for something the dog did. [2] It also isn't shorthand for a specific prediction of what the dog is likely to do next. [3] Nor do we know enough about the physiology of dogs for it to be an abbreviation for some statement about the dog's nervous system. [4] It is useful because of its connection with all of these things and because what it says about the dog corresponds in an informative way with similar statements about people. [5] We can make similar statements about machines. [6] Here is an extract from the instructions that came with an electric blanket. [7] "Place the control near the bed in a place that is neither hotter nor colder than the room itself. [8] If the control is placed on a radiator or radiant heated floors, it will 'think' the entire room is hot and will lower your blanket temperature, making your bed too cold. [9] If the control is placed on the window sill in a cold draft, it will 'think' the entire room is cold and will heat up your bed so it will be too hot." [10] Nevertheless, the blanket manufacturer was probably right in thinking that it would help some users. [11] The example of the thermostat is a very simple one. [12] Here's a somewhat fanciful example of a machine that might someday be encountered in daily life with more substantial mental qualities. [13] The automatic teller is another example. [14] It has beliefs like, 'There's enough money in the account,' and 'I don't give out that much money'. [15] A person who doesn't know Chinese memorizes a book of rules for manipulating Chinese characters. [16] These rules say nothing about the meaning of the characters, just how to compute with them. [17] We suppose that the rules result in a Chinese conversation so intelligent that the person giving and receiving the sentences can't tell him from an intelligent Chinese. [18] This is analogous to a computer, which only obeys its programming language, but can be programmed such that one can communicate with it in a different programming language, or in English. [19] I would argue that the set of rules understands Chinese, and, analogously, a computer program may be said to understand things, even if the computer does not.
