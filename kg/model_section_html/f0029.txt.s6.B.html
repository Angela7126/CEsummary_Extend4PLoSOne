[0] The SDSS catalog is cross-matched with FIRST, RC3, ROSAT, Stetson, and USNO-B as part of the pipeline processing. [1] Most objects are slow-moving so their displacement between observations is small compared to the average inter-object difference. [2] Near-Earth object apparent motions are typically large and so measurements must be within minutes for the techniques described here to detect object pairs. [3] The SDSS is observed in five spectral bands -- each band's observation occurs about a minute after the previous band. [4] Those five measurements allow cross-matching observations of objects with apparent motions of 0.01 to 10 arcminutes per minute (or a comparable number of degrees per day). [5] The SDSS processing pipeline looks for such objects and records their apparent velocities (in units of degrees per day) in the catalog. [6] When considering SDSS observations separated by days or years, only very slow-moving objects can be detected with the cross-match techniques here. [7] In general, a naive spatial match does not work for fastmoving objects. [8] Rather one must model the object's motion, and then predict where that object will be in the observational field. [9] Unfortunately, model uncertainties accumulate with time -- especially for fast moving nearearth objects. [10] But, it is sometimes the case that one wants a match table of the form (x, y, z) built from three surveys X, Y, Z where the match elements are the corresponding elements of the survey -- in general the problem involves more than three surveys or observations, but three is enough to demonstrate the issues. [11] Expressed in relational terms this is a full-outer spatial join among the N catalogs. [12] The full-outer part of that expression means that there may be zero, one, or many items that match for each bundle. [13] We call such a cross-match representations a pivoted cross-match (as opposed to a pair-table cross-match) because this representation is the pivot of the pairs table on the match-head and run number.
