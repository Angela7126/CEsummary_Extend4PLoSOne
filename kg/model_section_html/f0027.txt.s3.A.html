[0] Today, many scientists 'mine' available databases, looking for new patterns and discoveries, without performing experiments themselves. [1] However, this data-rich approach to science faces challenges. [2] Large data archives are becoming increasingly 'isolated' in the network sense since the speed of internet cannot keep up with the growth of scientific dataset. [3] Distributed computation may be a solution, which typically mean sending computations to the data rather than copying the data to your workstation. [4] But the management of distributed computations raises new questions of security, free access to public data and cost. [5] Are we reaching the limits of what one scientist, or one lab, can expect to achieve in data handling and analysis? [6] If so, this will have implications for how we review and publish our work. [7] For an example, if we want to reproduce the procedures of a data-ming paper in 20 years', we need to archiving both its data and its minging tools. [8] However, although some disciplines have created their own data archives, there is no standard for publishing large volumes of data until now.. [9] This increase the difficulty of the acquisition of large volume data. [10] Current databases still has much effect to do, such as encourage data sharing by rewarding, give public data creators and publishers credit, and enable archives to automatically provide provenance details. [11] Due to the sustained and rapid development of computers and scientific instruments, we will expect that this exponential growth in scientific data will continue for quite a long time.
