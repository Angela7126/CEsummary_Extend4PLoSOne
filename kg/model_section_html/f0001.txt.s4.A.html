[0] Citation is a kind of representation of selection and language use. [1] It is also a basic semantic link of constructing a complex text. [2] Therefore, it is necessary to understand citation when studying summarization. [3] Citation can be classified into explicit citation and implicit citation. [4] Explicit citations can be easily found in scientific papers and books. [5] These citations form citation networks which renders the source, the information and evolution of research areas. [6] Summaries of different scales can be obtained through zoom-in-and-zoom-out on a citation network. [7] Explicit citations also exist among webpages, i.e, hyperlink. [8] Implicit citation often used in fre texts and literature works. [9] Some implicit citation have mark words such as "someone says", while others just reuse other's sentences or clauses without any mark words. [10] Summarizing various citations can reach a notion of general citation. [11] Furthermore, a representation's intention and extension can also be defined by citations. [12] The extension of representation A consists of all representations that cite A and are cited by A. [13] The core representation renders the core idea of a representation, which has a high rank in the citation network. [14] The intention of representation is indicated by core representations and by citation from other representations. [15] Therefore, a representation can be composed by a set of representations. [16] For example, if we view entries in a dictionary as basic implicit citations to words, then any text has a set of basic citations. [17] So if we regard citation as the fundamental behavior and mechanism of summarization, the summarization S of a set of representations P is rendered by its intention Int(P) and extension Ext(B).
