[1] In this paper we proposed a method for automatic detection of cognates based on orthographic alignment.. [2] We employed the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm [ 29 ] for sequence alignment widely-used in computational biology and we used aligned pairs of words to extract rules for lexical changes occurring when words enter new languages.. [3] We applied our method on an automatically extracted dataset of cognates for four pairs of languages.. [4] As future work, we plan to extend our method on a few levels.. [5] In this paper we used a very simple substitution matrix for the alignment algorithm, but the method can be adapted to integrate historical information regarding language evolution.. [6] The substitution matrix for the alignment algorithm can be customized with language-specific information, in order to reflect the probability of a character to change into another.. [7] An important achievement in this direction belongs to Delmestri and Cristianini ( 2010 ) , who introduced PAM-like matrices, linguistic-inspired substitution matrices which are based on information regarding orthographic changes.. [8] We plan to investigate the contribution of using this type of substitution matrices for our method.. [9] We intend to investigate other approaches to string alignment, such as local alignment [ 33 ] , and other learning algorithms for discriminating between cognates and non-cognates.. [10] We plan to extend our analysis with more language-specific features, where linguistic knowledge is available.. [11] First, we intend to use the part of speech as an additional feature.. [12] We assume that some orthographic changes are dependent on the part of speech of the words.. [13] Secondly, we want to investigate whether accounting for the common ancestor language influences the results.. [14] We are interested to find out if the orthographic rules depend on the source language, or if they are rather specific to the target language.. [15] Finally, we plan to make a performance comparison on cognate pairs versus word-etymon pairs and to investigate false friends [ 27 ].. [16] We further intend to adapt our method for cognate detection to a closely related task, namely cognate production, i.e.,, given an input word w , a related language L and a set of learned rules for orthographic changes, to produce the cognate pair of w in L..
