[0] Negation words, such as no and not , play a fundamental role in modifying sentiment of textual expressions. [1] We will refer to a negation word as the negator and the text span within the scope of the negator as the argument. [2] Commonly used heuristics to estimate the sentiment of negated expressions rely simply on the sentiment of argument (and not on the negator or the argument itself. [3] We use a sentiment treebank to show that these existing heuristics are poor estimators of sentiment. [4] We then modify these heuristics to be dependent on the negators and show that this improves prediction. [5] Next, we evaluate a recently proposed composition model [] that relies on both the negator and the argument. [6] This model learns the syntax and semantics of the negator s argument with a recursive neural network. [7] We show that this approach performs better than those mentioned above. [8] In addition, we explicitly incorporate the prior sentiment of the argument and observe that this information can help reduce fitting errors. [9] Negation plays a fundamental role in modifying sentiment. [10] In the process of semantic composition, the impact of negators is complicated by the syntax and semantics of the text spans they modify. [11] This paper provides a comprehensive and quantitative study of the behavior of negators through a unified view of fitting human annotation. [12] We first measure the modeling capabilities of two influential heuristics on a sentiment treebank and find that they capture some effect of negation; however, extending these non-lexicalized models to be dependent on the negators improves the performance statistically significantly. [13] The detailed analysis reveals the differences in the behavior among negators, and we argue that they should always be modeled separately. [14] We further make the models to be dependent on the text being modified by negators, through adaptation of a state-of-the-art recursive neural network to incorporate the syntax and semantics of the arguments; we discover this further reduces fitting errors.
