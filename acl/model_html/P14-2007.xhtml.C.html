[0] The effort required for a human annotator to detect sentiment is not uniform for all texts, irrespective of his/her expertise. [1] We aim to predict a score that quantifies this effort, using linguistic properties of the text. [2] Our proposed metric is called Sentiment Annotation Complexity (SAC. [3] As for training data, since any direct judgment of complexity by a human annotator is fraught with subjectivity, we rely on cognitive evidence from eye-tracking. [4] The sentences in our dataset are labeled with SAC scores derived from eye-fixation duration. [5] Using linguistic features and annotated SACs, we train a regressor that predicts the SAC with a best mean error rate of 22.02% for five-fold cross-validation. [6] We also study the correlation between a human annotator s perception of complexity and a machine s confidence in polarity determination. [7] The merit of our work lies in (a) deciding the sentiment annotation cost in, for example, a crowdsourcing setting, (b) choosing the right classifier for sentiment prediction. [8] We presented a metric called Sentiment Annotation Complexity (SAC), a metric in SA research that has been unexplored until now. [9] First, the process of data preparation through eye tracking, labeled with the SAC score was elaborated. [10] Using this data set and a set of linguistic features, we trained a regression model to predict SAC. [11] Our predictive framework for SAC resulted in a mean percentage error of 22.02%, and a moderate correlation of 0.57 between the predicted and observed SAC values. [12] Finally, we observe a negative correlation between the classifier confidence scores and a SAC, as expected. [13] As a future work, we would like to investigate how SAC of a test sentence can be used to choose a classifier from an ensemble, and to determine the pre-processing steps (entity-relationship extraction, for example.
