[0] Sentiment analysis in a multilingual world remains a challenging problem, because developing language-specific sentiment lexicons is an extremely resource-intensive process. [1] Such lexicons remain a scarce resource for most languages. [2] In this paper, we address this lexicon gap by building high-quality sentiment lexicons for 136 major languages. [3] We integrate a variety of linguistic resources to produce an immense knowledge graph. [4] By appropriately propagating from seed words, we construct sentiment lexicons for each component language of our graph. [5] Our lexicons have a polarity agreement of 95.7% with published lexicons, while achieving an overall coverage of 45.2%. [6] We demonstrate the performance of our lexicons in an extrinsic analysis of 2,000 distinct historical figures Wikipedia articles on 30 languages. [7] Despite cultural difference and the intended neutrality of Wikipedia articles, our lexicons show an average sentiment correlation of 0.28 across all language pairs. [8] Our knowledge graph propagation is generally effective at producing useful sentiment lexicons. [9] Interestingly, the ratio of positive sentiment words is strongly connected with number of sentiment words it is noteworthy that English has the smallest ratio of positive lexicon terms. [10] The phenomenon possibly shows that many negative words reflecting cultural nuances do not translate wel. [11] We believe that this ratio can be considered as quality measurement of the propagation. [12] Similar approaches can be extended to other NLP tasks using different semantic links, specific dictionary and special seed words. [13] Future work will revolve around learning modifiers, negation terms, and various entity/sentiment attribution.
